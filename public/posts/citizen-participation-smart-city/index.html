Citizen Participation in the Smart City

March 15, 2026

Overview and Research Questions#

The Smart Nation Office in Singapore launched the Smart Nation Builder, a mobile exhibition designed to gather resident feedback on government digital services. While the initiative was aimed to promote inclusive civic participation, it was unclear who was actually engaging with the experience — and whether the format enabled meaningful feedback that could inform future decisions on civic tech.

I approached this case as if advising a civic technology team responsible for designing inclusive engagement experiences. The research project was thus designed to examine how participation was structured within the Smart Nation Builder, who was able or unable to participate, and how civic engagement initiatives like these could be improved to better support inclusive and actionable input from residents.

The project asked the following:

  1. How is participation structured by the Smart Nation Builder? What forms of participation are encouraged? Conversely, what is left out?
  2. Who are the imagined participants of the Smart Nation Builder—and who is left out?
  3. Given the above, how might civic engagement initiatives be better designed?

Methodology and Timeline#

  • Participant observation conducted between September 2022 and June 2023
  • To understand how participation was designed at the Smart Nation Builder, I conducted participant observation under two roles (in addition to being a researcher): a Smart Nation Ambassador—an official volunteer with the Smart Nation Office—and as a part-time employee at the Smart Nation Builder. These two roles gave me different positionalities from which to explore my research questions.
    • As a Smart Nation Ambassador, I registered for short, three hour stints at the Builder and reported to the Smart Nation Office employee stationed at the Builder. I was tasked to guide visitors to the Builder: helping them register and then bringing them around its 8 stations.
    • As a part-time employee, I worked with the agency contracted to run the Builder. Here, in addition to guiding visitors around the Builder, I also worked on setting and/or closing up the Builder on days that it was deployed.

Findings#

1. The Builder worked well for younger residents who were already comfortable with digital technologies…#

The Builder brought civic engagement into the residential areas, what local residents would familiarly call the “heartlands” of Singapore, which helped extend its reach. While this increased foot traffic, the Builder was nonetheless primarily designed for digitally-literate, English-speaking users. Its registration kiosk nudged visitors to use Singpass, the national digital ID, rather than manual entry; and its interactive screens assumed comfort with both touchscreen navigation and the English language.

For visitors who met these conditions, the experience was smooth: many were able to move through the truck independently, without requiring assistance from guides, and were easily able to provide qualitative feedback on ongoing digital initiatives when prompted.

2. …and less well for the elderly community.#

For visitors who fell outside of the Builder’s assumed user profile, the experience was marked by friction. Unfamiliarity with Singpass, whether due to a forgotten 6-pin password or difficultly with scanning a QR code, created barriers at registration.

More significantly, many elderly residents were not English-speaking, but instead communicated in Tamil, Malay, or one of several Chinese dialects. In practice, this meant that Smart Nation Ambassadors and Volunteers often had to guide visitors one-on-one through each station, translating content and, where needed, transcribing visitors’ responses into English to enter into the Builder’s system.

This also had broader operational consequences. During peak hours, staff had to be positioned either to accompany elderly visitors station by station or to stand ready at each of the eight stations to mediate and translate content, ultimately creating a strain on limited personnel.

3. Does feedback mediated through intermediaries still count as civic engagement?#

The frictions described above pointed to a relatively straight-forward design fix: while printed text could remain in English, content displayed on interactive touchscreens could be translated into Singapore’s other official languages (Malay, Tamil, and Mandarin).

More interestingly, they raised an important question of methodological rigour: if Builder staff members were actively mediating visitors’ engagements, what does that mean for the quality of the qualitative feedback collected? Put differently: does feedback collected this way still count as civic engagement, filtered as it were by the thought processes and words of an intermediary?

Yes and no. In one sense, yes: summarizing or synthesizing visitor responses (which was much of that the Builder staff were doing) still constitutes a form of resident feedback. One could go further and characterize staff as provocateurs: not merely reading questions aloud, but also prompting and even co-constructing responses with visitors. In doing so, their involvement may have produced richer commentary than what visitors would have offered independently.

That said, this describes an ideal scenario. In my observations, it was often the case that peak periods of high footfall frequently compromised feedback quality. Staff already managing crowd flow were incentivized to move visitors quickly through each station, resulting in abbreviated comments, in some cases, bypassing feedback sections entirely.

Side note: Language matters. A related and perhaps under-explored dimension of the Builder’s design assumptions concerns language not just as a practical, operational barrier, but also as a condition for meaningful self-expression. When residents are required to provide feedback in a language that they are not comfortable with, what is lost is not just accuracy but also nuance: the exact kind of specificity that makes qualitative feedback (and qualitative research) useful. A resident who is fluent in Tamil or Malay but only functional in English may be able to convey that a digital service has not been working well for them, but they are less likely to convey why, or to do so in a way that carries the texture of their lived experience. That the Builder was designed with English as its sole feedback language is thus not a neutral technical decision, instead, it encoded assumptions about who was expected to participate, and on which terms. Designing for mother-tongue input would not only have reduced friction, but would have also signalled that all residents’ voices were welcome in the registers that they were most comfortable in.

Impact and Recommendations: Next steps for designing civic engagement#

One immediate recommendation shared by myself and colleagues was for each station’s content to be translated into at least three additional languages. This would then include the other official languages of the city-state (Malay, Tamil, and Mandarin). Presenting content in residents’ mother tongues would have signalled a more welcome engagement environment, and would have reduced friction for both visitors and the staff tasked with mediating their experience.

Policy recommendations for civic engagement:

  1. Incorporate frontline staff perspectives into design feedback loops. The experiences of Builder staff, both volunteers and part-time workers, contain important diagnostic insights about where the initiative succeeded and where it fell short. Bringing these perspectives into contact with the developers responsible for the Builder’s design could surface pain points earlier. In my observations, this feedback loop was largely absent: the gap between operational staff and developers was significant, with the relationship primarily mediated through the contracting agency.
  2. Reconsider the role of digital technology in civic engagement projects. The Builder’s reliance on digital interfaces raises the question of when digital tools are actually the right choice. For more targeted feedback on specific initiatives, in-person formats such as focus groups may yield richer, more actionable input, particularly from populations less comfortable with digital interfaces.

Reflections#

Designing for broad participation often defaults to designing for a “typical” user. In this instance, the Builder illustrates some of the costs of that default. As with my reflections on digital payments, elderly users are not edge cases: their experiences reveal important truths about how digital change is received more broadly, and should inform design from the outset rather than something addressed as an afterthought.

In doing this research, I was struck by how open people were to conversation. Where the Builder worked best, it seemed to have happened almost incidentally, as a provocation for deeper dialogue. Prompts about specific ongoing projects like an ongoing digital health project, the National Steps Challenge, sparked organic conversations in which members of the public shared their experiences using wearables and even offered suggestions for the project’s improvement. This relational, conversational quality of engagement may be worth deliberately designing for in future initiatives, whether for civic engagement projects or otherwise.
