# Overview and Research Questions

Despite the rapid proliferation of AI ethics frameworks and guidelines, the voices of AI practitioners themselves remain largely absent from these conversations. While both academic and industry critics have raised concerns about “ethics washing”, where high-level principles serve more as reputational cover than regulatory guidance, the deeper problem may be structural. Practitioners are not simply struggling to understand abstract ethical language, instead they are operating within organizational environments that can crowd out ethical deliberation. 

This research asks: **What needs to change if we take seriously the organizational and operational challenges facing practitioners building and deploying AI systems?** 

Rather than treating the gap between principles and practices as a communication problem to be solved by clearer guidelines, this work proposes that closing it requires a different approach: one grounded in dialogue, distributed responsibility, and the lived experiences of AI practitioners.

# Methodology and Timeline

- Focus groups and facilitated workshops conducted between November 2019 and April 2020, with later sessions adapted to online formats due to COVID-19
- Participants drawn from three organizational contexts: a multinational technology firm, tech consultancies, and state-sponsored AI developers
- Workshops were structured in three stages: 1. practitioner self-reflection on their work and where the work sits in the AI development pipeline; 2. a guided discussion of ethics principles and their operational relevance; and 3. scenario-based exercises exploring how ethical challenges arise and are navigated
- Deliberately designed as a **dialogue rather than an audit:** we designed the methodology that prioritized creating a trusted space for honest reflection rather than measuring compliance or evaluating ethical competency

# Findings

## 1. The ethics gap is structural. 

Current AI ethics frameworks assume that clearer principles will lead to better practice — but this paper shows that the problem runs deeper. AI practitioners are not simply confused about abstract ethical language, they are also operating within organizational structures where meeting deadlines, satisfying contracts, and navigating power hierarchies consistently take precedence over ethical deliberation. Ethics, in this context, is something that happens around practitioners rather than with them. 

> But on a contract and a project for another company, the number one thing that is driving choices is meeting terms of the contract, there is little room for thinking about ethics, we’re not breaking rules, but it boils down to meeting deadlines and getting things ready… as long as nothing is flagrant[ly wrong], you do what needs to get done. (Workshop participant)

Workshop participants explicitly described how contract and deadline pressures left little room for ethical reflection. They noted that so long as nothing was “flagrant”, getting things done took priority. This sentiment was consistent across focus groups spanning multinational tech firms, private consultancies, and state-sponsored developers. 

**Implication**: Interventions aimed at improving AI ethics, that is closing the gap between AI principles and practice, cannot stop at better communication of principles. They must account for the organizational conditions (i.e., resource constraints, incentive structures, and power dynamics) that determine whether ethics ever reaches the decision-making table in the first place.

## 2. Mutualized ethical discussions have higher impact than mandated ones. 

Top-down ethics frameworks are typically directed at senior management and organizations as a whole, but the study points out that this can have the effect of bypassing the engineers, developers, and frontline practitioners who make the most consequential day-to-day decisions. Ethical responsibility, when it is not deliberately distributed, can by default then disappear into abstraction.

In the discussions, participants reported uncertainty about where their own ethical responsibilities began and ended within their teams. Responsibility attribution was thus unclear, and without a shared language and mechanism for practitioners to locate themselves within a broader ethical accountability structure. 

**Implication**: Effective AI governance frameworks need to move beyond organizational-level compliance and engage practitioners and teams at the *project level*. Shared ownership of ethical outcomes — built through dialogue between stakeholders and practitioners — is more likely to produce internalized responsibility rather than mandates.

## 3. Conversation can function as a regulatory tool. 

Through the project, it became clear that facilitated dialogue structured around real operational decisions rather than abstract principles can function as a form of ethical regulation in its own right. By creating space for practitioners to reflect honestly on where ethics breaks down in their workflows, conversation disrupts taken-for-granted practices and enables more engagement with ethical responsibility than compliance training achieves. 

The workshops conducted for this project reveal consistent themes across different organizations: confusion over ethical responsibility, the inaccessibility of ethical language, and a genuine desire for tools that make ethics operationally meaningful. Critically, these insights only surfaced in a facilitated, trust-building conversation.

**Implication**: Given that investing in facilitated ethics conversations at the team level can produce more durable change than expanding compliance infrastructure, the study suggests that organizations and regulators should consider **structured practitioner dialogue** as a legitimate governance mechanism in the production of AI. At the same time, one question here is how to “scale” these dialogues both within organizations and across them.
