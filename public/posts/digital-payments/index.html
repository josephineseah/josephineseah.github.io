Digital Payments in the Speedy Smart City

March 16, 2026

Overview and Research Questions#

Digital payments have made steady progress in recent years and we are now accustomed to non-cash modes of payments: from NFC-enabled transactions to scanning QR codes to sending bitcoin. These new infrastructures of payments have changed mental models of everyday transactions in the urban city: whether at the grocery store or in restaurants, people reach for their phones rather than cash, expecting transactions to be both digitally mediated and speedy.

At the same time, who are the assumed users of these systems, and at whose expense? This project looks at digital payment adoption in Singapore through the lens of inclusion and exclusion. In it, I focus not just on whether residents are participating in the city’s digital infrastructure, but on what terms and with what costs. More specifically, this project asks:

  1. What kinds of users and behaviours do digital payment systems assume, and what happens to residents whose lives do not fit within that mold?
  2. Where does the logic of efficiency and convenience come into tension with how residents actually experience and make meaning from everyday transactions?
  3. When these systems fail, who bears the cost, and what does recovery look like?

Methodology and Timeline#

  • Interviews were held between January-February 2025
  • I conducted semi-structured in-depth interviews with 29 Singapore residents
    • Interviews were between 30-90 minutes
    • Thematic coding using grounded theory, supplemented by reflexive memo-writing, using Altas.ti

Findings#

1. Digital payments actively produce a mobile, transactional, and always-on user.#

Across the interviews, it was clear that residents were not just using digital payments. Instead, they were reshaping their daily routines around them. When residents describe using digital payments to coordinate their commutes, their orders, and their waiting time, they are doing more than optimizing for convenience. They are, often without realizing it, synchronizing themselves with the broader rhythms of the city. Digital payments, in this sense, are a key part of how the smart city produces a particular kind of urban user: mobile, transactional, and always-on.

“McDonald’s always takes time to prepare your food, especially at the outlet at my neighbourhood, so I order when I’m on the train, and when I read, I get it. So it’s more convenient, I don’t have to wait so long.”

The McDonald’s example above captures this well. The user ordering food on the train is exercising agency: making her day flow better, reducing what she feels to be waiting time. But she is also, in the same gesture, slotting herself into a system designed to produce this behaviour: frictionless consumption choreographed across space and time. We can thus situate digital payments within the smart city as infrastructures that produce the urban experience and shape the urban denizen’s very movements.

Implication: Seamless digital experiences are rarely neutral. They are built around an assumed user: mobile, digital natives who are willing and able to reorganize their daily routines around transactional convenience. For most users, this alignment goes unnoticed precisely because it works. Yet for those who cannot or choose not to move at this tempo, perhaps due to age, access, education, or simply a preference for a different rhythm in daily life, the system does not align with how they live. For product teams, this then becomes a prompt for asking whose behaviour seamlessness implicitly assumes, and who is left out when that assumption fails to hold in reality.

2. Users are not always optimizing for efficiency, designing as if it is can strip meaning from social experiences.#

Most users said digital payments were simply better. For most interactions, they were right. Digital payments are convenient and low-effort. They reduce the physical weight of carrying cash and coins and the mental stress of on-the-spot calculations. For many of my interviewees, digital payments were an efficient modus operandi in the city.

Nonetheless, such efficiencies were not always prioritized nor welcome. We may live in cities, but our lives, even when dealing with money, are not always about optimizing for efficiency. In my interviews, it was also the case that assumptions about digital payments as faster and more efficient broke down the moment that the exchange of money is seen as more than just the simple movement of value from one hand to another.

For residents, there are moments when the act of giving money is inseparable from its meaning. For instance, residents I talked to would often comment on the difference between an electronic red package (locally known as “e-angpows”, a service provided by many local banks) and a physical one; or the difference between a simple bank transfer and a physical envelop of filled with cash (a common wedding gift):

There’s no feeling in [using e-angpows], this whole thing just falls back to tradition. It’s not about convenience; it’s about the meaning behind [the practice]. So long as the meaning is there, then we should stick to that. We cannot simply convert everything to electronics, there is no value in that. (emphasis added)

Similarly, one resident told me that she enjoyed the feeling of a “thick” envelope of money given to couples at weddings:

I still like that feeling. If everything is digital, then no warmth in the exchange.

These expressions are not ones of technophobia or nostalgia; rather they are observations about what gets lost when the logic of efficiency is prioritized.

Implications: Products built around efficiency and convenience rest on an implicit assumption: that what users want from an interaction is to complete it fast. This study shows where that assumption breaks down. For some interactions, such as gifting money at weddings or handing a loved one a red packet during Lunar New Year, the act itself carries meaning that does not translate easily into the move to frictionless transactions. For designers, this highlights the importance of the distinction between understanding what an interaction means to a user and how an interaction unfolds. This is the difference between designing for completion and designing for the full weight of what an exchange is doing for someone.

3. Digital inclusion doesn’t stop at access, we need to consider who (or, which groups) absorb(s) the cost of breakdown.#

By many measures, Singapore’s shift towards cashless payments has been a success. Across interviews, contactless payments had become unremarkable for most residents: clearly woven into the fabric of daily life without most friction or thought. The “last mile” problem that once loomed large in digital payment adoption has, for many, disappeared.

On the other hand, this story of seamless integration sits alongside another one. Rising scam cases in Singapore have made digital transactions feel precarious for one particular group: the elderly. For seniors already navigating the edges of digital participation, the threat of scams looms. For some, it can be enough to pull them out of many forms of digital participation, for others, it can leave them suspended in a state of anxiety and distrust that makes ordinary urban participation feel risky.

When digital payment systems fail, be it through scams, fraud, or breach of trust, the fallout is not shared equally. For residents who were already navigating digital life with less confidence, the aftermath can be paralyzing: a withdrawal from transactions, a distrust of systems that others use without a second thought, and an exclusion from broader urban participation. Consider, for instance, the experience shared below:

What happened was: my mother thought her friend called her asking for money. The caller gave her a bank account number. She transferred it across and then couldn’t contact her friend anymore. Finally, through social media, she contacted her friend and asked, “Why didn’t you answer your phone?” He said, “I’ve changed my phone number, and I never asked you for money.” Then she reported the incident to the bank and the police, and the police said, “You knew, you were given the bank account number, and you knowingly transferred. Yes, it’s to the wrong person, but you knowingly transferred, so say bye bye to your money.” And it was four figures, so it’s not a small amount. And then for the bank to just tell her to just “say bye bye to your money”. It’s just very, very painful. So… it just happened too fast Everything happens so fast.

Here, then continues continues, drawing a clear distinction between financial and emotional consequences and her concerns of the latter on her mother’s ability to fully participate in the city:

Afterwards, you know, I transferred her the same amount and said, “Ah, never mind, take it that I paid your friend.” But she still feels the pain of losing that money, even though she didn’t lose the actual money because I have already given it back to her. But she feels the pain, and the worst thing is she feels the stupidity of doing that, and she feels even more scared of this world now. And her confidence level has dropped. For the longest time she was saying, “I’ll just use cash, I’ll just use cash.” I told her, no no no no, you cannot go backwards, you must go with the [flow]… And she’s still very healthy! I think she still has 10 years, if not 20 years more to go. And at this rate, if she doesn’t learn now, she’s going to be left, left behind by the whole world. But every time you hit her with something like that, she, she… you know, it hits her confidence. It hits her… her pride. Everything… I feel sad, I’m about to tear up because I lived through it with her, and it’s been so hard to pull her out.

Implication: Digital inclusion is often measured at the point of adoption: did the user gain access, did they start using the system? This study points to a different and arguably neglected metric: what happens when a system fails, and who bears the cost of that failure? For elderly users already navigating digital life with less confidence, a single scam incident can be enough to undo years of adoption. The quote above illustrates this at a logistical level and at an emotional one as well: the loss of confidence, the shame, and the withdrawal from participation. For teams working on trust, safety, and digital inclusion then, this changes the core question from, “How can we get users onto the platform?” to “How can we ensure that people most vulnerable to harm are also protected when things fail or go wrong?”

So what?#

1. Design for the full range of what transactions mean to users, not just how they complete them.#

The red packet instance above is just one example of a broader phenomenon, indicating that there are categories of exchange where meaning is inseparable from the act itself, and where the logic of efficiency works against what users value. Gifting money during Lunar New Year or at weddings is a visible example, but unlikely to be the only ones. Other festive transfers, religious donations, informal lending practices, and intergenerational money flows can all similarly carry social and cultural weight that frictionless digital systems are arguably not equipped to hold.

For product and policy teams, this finding points to a design orientation that looks beyond optimizing for task completion. In other words, before digitizing a transaction category, it is worth asking: what does this exchange mean to the people performing it, and what would be lost if that exchange was reduced to a transfer of value? Often, perhaps, the answer will be nothing: money is changing hands in an instrumental manner. Sometimes, however, the act itself matters, and designing as if it doesn’t will produce tools that do not speak to users lived experiences.

2. Expand digital inclusion frameworks to account for recovery, in addition to access.#

Current approaches to digital inclusion in Singapore have been largely preventive in orientation. ScamShield, police hotlines, and advertisements warning residents about scam types are focused on upstream interventions: to stop scams before they occur. Yet the prevalence of scams in the city suggests that prevention is not sufficient.

What is comparatively underdeveloped is an infrastructure for recovery. More than just financial restitution (which remains difficult to secure in many cases), the study points to a subtler and more durable form of harm: the loss of confidence, an internalized shame, and sometimes, the withdrawal from digital and urban participation. For elderly users in particular, a single scam encounter can undo years of careful digital adoption; implicating not just the individual but also their household, as family members navigate the emotional and practical consequences together.

Designing for recovery means taking these downstream effects seriously as a policy and a product problem. This could include cleaner and more compassionate institutional responses at the point of harm, peer support structures, community-level digital literacy (and confidence) programs, and follow-up touchpoints that treat scam victims as people in need of re-engagement. The goal not just to stop people from being scammed, but to ensure that when they do occur, they do not foreclose digital participation in a city that increasingly relies on it.

3. Audit who the assumed user is, and design explicitly for those who fall outside that assumption.#

What is clear from this study is that digital systems are built around an assumed user that many residents do not (always) resemble. In the case of digital payments, the assumed user is mobile while always being connected, one who treats transactions as instrumental and are resilient enough to absorb the failures of these systems without lasting consequences. For residents who fit into this profile, the city works for them and life simply goes on. For those who do not, the same city can feel exclusionary.

To build better products and policies, make this assumption explicit, and revisit it regularly to understand and address the ways in which inclusion and exclusion in digital programs can change over time.

Reflections#

This was my favourite mini-project of my doctoral research, no doubt partially arising from a clearer sense of knowing what I needed to do and having the experience of reaching out to participants before which helped to build my confidence that I could do the work within the time that I had.

I also enjoyed reflecting on the relationship between what Ned Rossiter calls “logistical media”—the infrastructure and labour that sustains the movement of people and commodities across space—and the temporal nature of smart cities. Put simply, can we think of the adoption of NFC-enabled payment systems and QR-code payment infrastructures as part of a system that structures our time in the city? How does that change the nature of city participation? How does that change our experience with time? Thinking about it this way helped me formulate my thinking about the nature of time in the city and it also helped me recognize the different registers of inclusion and exclusion arising from digital payment use.

That said, if I were to conduct this research again, I would have focused on just one group of residents: the elderly in Singapore. Over the course of the project, I came to see that their experience best captures the shifting experience that I was most interested in: the move from cash payments to the current ubiquity of cashless payments. This group also experiences, perhaps most explicitly, the structural (and temporal) pressures of digital payments, and in that sense more research could be done with this group to really make headway into more inclusive design.
